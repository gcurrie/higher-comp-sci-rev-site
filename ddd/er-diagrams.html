Entity-Relationship (ER) Diagrams

An ER diagram shows the entities in a database and the relationships between them. It is drawn from the analysis before the actual tables are created.

ER Diagram Notation

  • Rectangles represent entities (tables)
  • Lines represent relationships between entities
  • Crow's foot notation (or just labels) shows the type of relationship
  • The relationship name is written on the line

Relationship Types

TypeNotationExample
One-to-One (1:1) — 1 ——— 1 — A person has one passport; a passport belongs to one person
One-to-Many (1:M) — 1 ———< M — A teacher teaches many pupils; each pupil has one form teacher
Many-to-Many (M:N) — M >———< N — A pupil studies many subjects; each subject is studied by many pupils
ℹ️
A many-to-many relationship must be resolved by creating a linking table (also called a junction or bridge table) before the database can be implemented. The linking table has a compound primary key made from the two foreign keys.
Resolving a Many-to-Many: Pupil — Subject

A pupil studies many subjects AND a subject is studied by many pupils. To resolve this:

  • Create a Enrolment linking table
  • Enrolment has: PupilID (FK → Pupil) and SubjectID (FK → Subject)
  • Together, PupilID + SubjectID form the compound primary key of Enrolment
  • This becomes two 1:M relationships: Pupil —< Enrolment, Subject —< Enrolment

Three or More Entities

At Higher level, databases must involve three or more linked entities. This is a key difference from the simpler two-table examples seen at National 5.

Example Scenario — School Library System

Entities: Member, Book, Loan

  • A Member can make many Loans
  • A Book can appear in many Loans (different copies at different times)
  • Each Loan involves exactly one Member and one Book
  • Relationships: Member 1:M Loan, Book 1:M Loan

The Loan table acts as a linking entity between Member and Book, storing the date borrowed, date due, and date returned.

Entity-Occurrence Diagrams

An entity-occurrence diagram (also called a crow's foot instance diagram or occurrence diagram) shows actual example instances of entities and how specific occurrences relate to each other. It confirms you understand the cardinality of a relationship by showing it with real data.

While an ER diagram shows the type of relationship (e.g. "one teacher teaches many pupils"), an entity-occurrence diagram shows actual examples of that relationship in action.

In the exam you may be given a partially completed entity-occurrence diagram and asked to complete it, or asked to draw one to show you understand the relationship between two specific entities.

Compound Keys

A compound key (also called a composite key) is a primary key made of two or more attributes that together uniquely identify a record. No single attribute alone is sufficient to uniquely identify a row.

When Is a Compound Key Used?

Compound keys arise naturally in linking tables that resolve many-to-many relationships. For example, in an Enrolment table linking Pupil and Subject:

Enrolment Table — Compound Primary Key
Table: Enrolment
┌─────────────┬───────────┬──────────────┐
│ PupilID (PK)│SubjectID  │ Grade        │
│             │    (PK)   │              │
├─────────────┼───────────┼──────────────┤
│ P001        │ MATHS     │ A            │
│ P001        │ PHYSICS   │ B            │   ← same pupil, different subject
│ P002        │ MATHS     │ C            │   ← different pupil, same subject
│ P002        │ PHYSICS   │ A            │
└─────────────┴───────────┴──────────────┘

Primary Key = (PupilID, SubjectID) combined
  • PupilID alone is not unique (a pupil takes many subjects — appears multiple times)
  • SubjectID alone is not unique (many pupils take the same subject)
  • PupilID + SubjectID together are unique — a particular pupil only enrols in a particular subject once
ℹ️
When drawing an ER diagram with a many-to-many relationship, always resolve it with a linking table that has a compound primary key consisting of the two foreign keys.
