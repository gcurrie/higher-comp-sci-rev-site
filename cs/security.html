Computer Misuse Act 1990

The Computer Misuse Act 1990 is the main UK law covering illegal use of computers and computer systems. It defines three specific offences:

Offence 1 — Unauthorised Access to Computer Material

Accessing any computer, data or program without permission. This includes guessing or using another person's password to log into their account.

Maximum penalty: 12 months imprisonment and/or unlimited fine

Offence 2 — Unauthorised Access with Intent to Commit Further Offences

Accessing a computer without permission as a stepping stone to committing a more serious crime — for example, breaking into a bank's database with the intent to commit fraud.

Maximum penalty: 5 years imprisonment and/or unlimited fine

Offence 3 — Unauthorised Modification of Computer Material

Making unauthorised changes to data or programs — including installing malware, deleting files, changing records, or spreading a virus. The intent must be to impair operation or prevent access.

Maximum penalty: 10 years imprisonment and/or unlimited fine

💡
Exam questions may ask you to identify which offence a described scenario falls under. The key differences are: Offence 1 = access only; Offence 2 = access + intent for further crime; Offence 3 = modifying data/programs without permission.

Tracking Cookies

A cookie is a small text file stored on your computer by a website. A tracking cookie is specifically designed to monitor and record your browsing behaviour — often across multiple websites — for the purposes of advertising, analytics, or data collection.

How Tracking Cookies Work

  1. You visit a website that includes third-party advertising content (e.g. a Google Ads banner)
  2. The advertising company's server places a tracking cookie in your browser
  3. When you visit any other site that uses the same advertising company, they read the cookie and recognise you
  4. They build a profile of the sites you visit, what you search for, what you buy
  5. This profile is used to serve targeted adverts, or sold to data brokers

What Data Can Be Collected

  • Which websites you have visited and how often
  • What products or pages you have viewed
  • Your approximate location (from IP address)
  • Your device type, browser and operating system
  • Search terms used

Privacy Implications

  • Users may not be aware they are being tracked
  • Data can be shared with or sold to third parties without explicit consent
  • Creates detailed personal profiles that can be used for manipulative advertising
  • UK/EU law (GDPR, PECR) requires websites to get consent before placing tracking cookies — this is why cookie consent banners appear

Denial of Service (DOS) Attacks

A Denial of Service (DoS) attack is a deliberate attempt to make a website or online service unavailable to its intended users by overwhelming it with a flood of fake requests.

How a DOS Attack Works

A web server can only handle a limited number of simultaneous requests. If an attacker sends far more requests than the server can cope with:

  • The server becomes overloaded and cannot respond to legitimate requests
  • The service becomes very slow or completely unavailable
  • Legitimate users are denied access to the service

Distributed DOS (DDoS)

A Distributed DoS (DDoS) attack uses many computers simultaneously — often a botnet (a network of compromised computers controlled remotely, without their owners' knowledge). This makes the attack much harder to block because traffic comes from thousands of different IP addresses.

Symptoms of a DOS Attack

  • Website becomes extremely slow or unresponsive
  • Service is completely unavailable
  • Legitimate users receive timeout errors
  • Server logs show millions of requests from the same or many different IP addresses

Effects and Costs

EffectExample cost/consequence
Lost revenueAn e-commerce site loses all sales during the attack
Reputational damageCustomers lose trust in a site that is frequently unavailable
Recovery costsPaying for DDoS mitigation services, extra server capacity, security staff time
Ransom paymentAttackers may demand payment to stop the attack
Legal liabilityIf the site holds customer data, prolonged downtime may breach SLAs or data protection obligations

Why DOS Attacks Are Carried Out

  • Financial extortion — attackers demand a ransom to stop
  • Competition — unethical competitors disrupt a rival's service
  • Hacktivism — political protest against an organisation
  • Revenge or vandalism — targeting an organisation the attacker dislikes

Encryption

Encryption is the process of scrambling data so that it can only be read by someone with the correct key. It protects data in transit (e.g. over the internet) and at rest (e.g. stored on a server).

Public and Private Keys

Public key encryption (asymmetric encryption) uses a pair of mathematically linked keys:

🔓 Public Key
  • Shared openly — anyone can see it
  • Used to encrypt data being sent to the key's owner
  • Cannot decrypt data it has encrypted
🔑 Private Key
  • Kept secret by the owner — never shared
  • Used to decrypt data encrypted with the matching public key
  • Only the owner can decrypt messages sent to them
How Public Key Encryption Works — Step by Step
  1. Alice wants to send a secure message to Bob
  2. Bob publishes his public key openly
  3. Alice encrypts her message using Bob's public key
  4. The encrypted message is sent over the internet — even if intercepted, it cannot be read
  5. Bob uses his private key (only he has it) to decrypt the message
  6. Only Bob can read the message — even Alice cannot decrypt it once encrypted

Digital Certificates

A digital certificate is an electronic document that proves the identity of a website or organisation and confirms that its public key is genuine. Certificates are issued by a trusted third party called a Certificate Authority (CA) (e.g. DigiCert, Let's Encrypt).

What a Digital Certificate Contains

  • The organisation's name and website domain
  • The organisation's public key
  • The name of the issuing Certificate Authority
  • The certificate's expiry date
  • The CA's digital signature (proving the certificate is genuine)

Why Certificates Are Needed

Without certificates, an attacker could intercept traffic and substitute their own public key (a man-in-the-middle attack). The certificate proves that the public key you receive really belongs to the website you intended to visit, not an impersonator.

When a website has a valid certificate, browsers show a padlock icon in the address bar. The connection uses HTTPS (HTTP Secure).

Digital Signatures

A digital signature is a way of verifying that a document or message:

  1. Was created by the claimed sender (authenticity)
  2. Has not been altered since it was signed (integrity)

How Digital Signatures Work

  1. The sender creates a hash of the document (a fixed-size fingerprint of the content)
  2. The hash is encrypted using the sender's private key — this produces the digital signature
  3. The signed document (original + signature) is sent to the recipient
  4. The recipient decrypts the signature using the sender's public key — this reveals the original hash
  5. The recipient also creates their own hash of the received document
  6. If the two hashes match, the document is genuine and unmodified
Why This Works

Only the sender can create a valid signature (only they have the private key). Anyone can verify it (public key is openly available). If the document is changed after signing, the hashes will not match — tampering is immediately detected.
